### Gui Cang (歸藏) in English

The **Gui Cang** (also known as **Gui Cang Yi** or "Returning to the Hidden" in English translation) is one of the three ancient Chinese divination texts collectively referred to as the "Three Yi" (三易), alongside the **Lian Shan** (連山) and the **Zhou Yi** (周易, commonly known as the I Ching or Book of Changes). It represents a distinct tradition of Yi divination, emphasizing themes of return, concealment, and the earth's nurturing qualities, in contrast to the more heaven-oriented Zhou Yi. Below, I detail its background, history, current status, supporting evidence, objectives, and purpose based on historical records, archaeological findings, and scholarly research.

#### Background
The Gui Cang is rooted in ancient Chinese cosmology and divination practices, which date back to the Neolithic period but formalized during the Shang (Yin) Dynasty (c. 1600–1046 BCE). Traditional accounts attribute its origins to the Yellow Emperor (Huangdi, c. 2697–2597 BCE) or to the divine sage Fu Xi (伏羲), who is credited with creating the eight trigrams (bagua). However, it is more commonly associated with the Shang Dynasty, hence its alternative name "Yin Yi" (殷易 or Shang Yi).

The name "Gui Cang" derives from the idea of "returning to concealment" or "all things returning and hiding in the earth." As explained by Han Dynasty scholar Zheng Xuan (鄭玄, 127–200 CE), it signifies "wanwu mo bu gui er cang yu zhong" (萬物莫不歸而藏於中), emphasizing the earth's role as a repository for all things, aligning with yin (陰) principles of receptivity, fertility, and hidden potential. Unlike the Zhou Yi, which starts with the Qian (乾, heaven/masculine) hexagram, the Gui Cang begins with Kun (坤, earth/feminine), reflecting a matriarchal or earth-centered worldview possibly influenced by Shang culture's emphasis on ancestor worship, fertility rites, and goddess figures (e.g., connections to myths in the Shan Hai Jing or Classic of Mountains and Seas).

The text consists of 64 hexagrams (like the Zhou Yi), but with different sequences, interpretations, and divination methods. It uses unchanging lines (7 and 8) for omens, rather than changing lines (6 and 9) in the Zhou Yi, and its line statements often incorporate mythological narratives involving figures like Hou Yi (后羿), Chang'e (嫦娥), and Mu Tianzi (穆天子).

#### History
- **Ancient Origins and Transmission**: According to the Zhou Li (周禮 or Rites of Zhou), the royal diviners (taibu) managed three Yi systems: Lian Shan (Xia Dynasty), Gui Cang (Shang Dynasty), and Zhou Yi (Zhou Dynasty). The Gui Cang was purportedly used during the Shang era for state divinations, reflecting a society focused on earth deities and cyclical return. By the early Han Dynasty (206 BCE–220 CE), it was already fragmented; Huan Tan (桓譚, c. 43 BCE–28 CE) noted it contained about 4,300 characters, shorter than the Lian Shan. It survived in quotations in texts like the Taiping Yulan (太平御覽) and Yiwen Leiju (藝文類聚).

- **Loss and Partial Recovery**: The Gui Cang largely disappeared after the Han, with only scattered fragments preserved in later encyclopedias. By the Song Dynasty (960–1279 CE), only three sections—"Chu Jing" (初經, basic hexagrams), "Qi Mu Jing" (齊母經, mythological divinations), and "Ben Shi" (本蓍, yarrow stalk methods)—were known. It was considered lost by the Southern Song, leading scholars like Zhu Zhen (朱震) and Li Guo (李過) to reconstruct parts from citations in their works (e.g., Han Shang Yi Zhuan and Xi Xi Yi Shuo).

- **Qing Dynasty Reconstructions**: In the Qing Dynasty (1644–1912), scholars like Ma Guohan (馬國翰) compiled fragments in Yuhan Shanfang Jiyi Shu (玉函山房輯佚書), gathering about 500 characters from ancient sources. This became the standard "transmitted version" (輯佚本).

- **Modern Archaeological Breakthrough**: In 1993, over 800 bamboo slips were excavated from Tomb No. 15 at Wangjiatai (王家台), Jingzhou, Hubei Province (dated to the late Warring States to early Qin, c. 3rd century BCE). About 394 slips contained Yi divination texts, identified as a version of the Gui Cang. This "Qin Jian Gui Cang" (秦簡歸藏) confirmed the authenticity of transmitted fragments, revealing mythological hexagram statements and a Kun-first sequence.

#### Current Status
The Gui Cang remains a "lost" text in its complete form, with no full surviving manuscript. Research relies on:
- Transmitted compilations (e.g., Ma Guohan's version, about 500–1,000 characters).
- The Wangjiatai bamboo slips (c. 4,000 characters, but fragmented; not fully sequenced or intact).
- Ongoing scholarly editions: As of 2026, recent works include Xin Yamin's Gui Cang Yanjiu (歸藏研究, 2025, People's Publishing House), which synthesizes post-1993 studies, and digital reconstructions on platforms like Wikisource. No new major excavations have been reported since 1993, but digital humanities and AI-assisted paleography continue to refine readings (e.g., debates on hexagram names like "Jie" for Yu or "San" for Jia Ren).

Current research focuses on its mythological content, links to the Shan Hai Jing, and comparisons with the Zhou Yi. It is studied in academic fields like Sinology, archaeology, and comparative mythology, but not widely used for practical divination today, unlike the Zhou Yi.

#### Supporting Evidence
- **Textual Citations**: Fragments in Han and later texts (e.g., Zheng Xuan's commentaries) match the Qin slips, proving pre-Han existence.
- **Archaeological Finds**: The 1993 Wangjiatai slips directly corroborate transmitted versions (e.g., hexagram statements like the Chang'e myth in Gui Mei hexagram). Scholars like Wang Mingqin and Li Ling have released interpretations, confirming about 90% overlap in hexagram names with the Zhou Yi.
- **Comparative Studies**: Similarities with Mawangdui silk manuscripts (1973) and other Warring States Yi texts support its antiquity. Recent papers (e.g., Anke Hein's 2019 study on authenticity) use excavated texts to validate the Gui Cang against forgery accusations.
- **Mythological Correlations**: Hexagrams reference myths in the Mu Tianzi Zhuan (穆天子傳) and Huainanzi (淮南子), aligning with Shang oracle bone inscriptions' divination style.

No evidence suggests it was a post-Han forgery; the Qin slips date to before the Han book burnings (213 BCE).

#### Objectives and Purpose
- **Objectives**: The Gui Cang aimed to provide a system for divination (zhan shi, 占筮) using yarrow stalks or other methods to interpret omens for personal, agricultural, military, or state decisions. It focused on unchanging lines for stability predictions, differing from the Zhou Yi's emphasis on change.
- **Purpose**: Philosophically, it promoted a yin-centered cosmology, emphasizing earth's receptive power, cyclical return, and concealment as paths to harmony. In Shang context, it served ritual and governance, integrating myths to explain cosmic order. Unlike the Zhou Yi's Confucian moral lessons, the Gui Cang was more shamanistic, linking divination to ancestral and natural forces. Its purpose was practical (guiding actions) and cosmological (understanding yin-yang balance), influencing later Daoist and folk traditions.

In summary, the Gui Cang offers a window into pre-Zhou Chinese thought, highlighting earth's primacy over heaven, and remains a key artifact for understanding ancient divination diversity.

### 歸藏（英文：Gui Cang）詳細解釋（繁體中文）

**歸藏**（又稱**歸藏易**）是中國古代「三易」之一，與**連山**（連山易）和**周易**（易經）並稱。它代表一種獨特的易學傳統，強調「歸藏」與大地的孕育特質，與以天為主的周易形成對比。以下詳細說明其背景、歷史、當前狀態、支持證據、目標與目的，基於歷史記載、考古發現及學術研究。

#### 背景
歸藏源於中國古代宇宙觀與占卜實踐，可追溯至新石器時代，但於商（殷）朝（約前1600–1046年）正式化。傳統上歸屬黃帝（約前2697–2597年）或伏羲氏，伏羲被視為創造八卦的聖人。然而，它更常與商朝相關，故又名「殷易」。

名稱「歸藏」源自「萬物歸而藏於中」的概念，漢代鄭玄解釋為「萬物莫不歸而藏於中」，強調大地作為萬物歸宿，體現陰（陰柔、包容、潛藏）的原則。與周易以乾卦（天、陽剛）為首不同，歸藏以坤卦（地、陰柔）開頭，反映商文化重視母系、地母崇拜、祖先祭祀與女神形象（如與《山海經》神話連結）。

文本由64卦組成（與周易相同），但卦序、解釋與占法不同。它重視不變爻（七、八）為占，卦辭常融入神話敘事，如后羿、嫦娥、穆天子等。

#### 歷史
- **古代起源與傳承**：《周禮》記載周朝太卜掌三易：連山（夏易）、歸藏（殷易）、周易（周易）。歸藏據傳用於商朝國家占卜，反映重視大地循環的社會。由漢初已碎片化，桓譚《新論》記其約4300言，較連山簡短。存於如《太平御覽》、《藝文類聚》等後世引文。

- **亡佚與部分復原**：漢後漸亡佚，至宋代僅存三篇——「初經」（基本卦序）、「齊母經」（神話占卜）、「本蓍」（蓍草法）。南宋完全失傳，學者如朱震、李過從引文輯錄（如《漢上易傳》、《西溪易說》）。

- **清代輯佚**：清代馬國翰於《玉函山房輯佚書》匯集約500字碎片，成為標準「傳世輯本」。

- **現代考古突破**：1993年湖北荊州王家台15號秦墓出土800餘枚竹簡（戰國晚至秦初，約前3世紀），其中394枚為易占類，確認為歸藏版本。「秦簡歸藏」證實傳世碎片真實，揭示神話卦辭與坤首序。

#### 當前狀態
歸藏完整本已亡佚，研究依賴：
- 傳世輯本（如馬國翰版，約500–1000字）。
- 王家台竹簡（約4000字，但殘缺；尚未完整排序）。
- 近期著作：至2026年，新作如辛亞民《歸藏研究》（2025年，人民出版社），彙總1993年後成果；數位重構見維基文庫等。自1993年無重大新出土，但數位人文與AI古文字學持續精進釋讀（如卦名「介」為豫、「散」為家人之辯）。

當前研究聚焦神話內容、與《山海經》連結、周易比較。屬漢學、考古、比較神話領域，但不同周易般廣泛用於實占。

#### 支持證據
- **文本引述**：漢及後世引文（如鄭玄註）與秦簡吻合，證實前漢存在。
- **考古發現**：1993年王家台簡直接互證傳世本（如嫦娥神話於歸妹卦）。學者如王明欽、李零釋文，確認卦名與周易90%相近。
- **比較研究**：與1973年馬王堆帛書及其他戰國易文類似，支持古遠。近期論文（如Anke Hein 2019年真實性研究）用出土文獻駁偽書說。
- **神話相關**：卦涉及《穆天子傳》、《淮南子》神話，與商甲骨文占卜風格一致。

無證據顯示後漢偽造；秦簡早於焚書坑儒（前213年）。

#### 目標與目的
- **目標**：提供占筮系統，用蓍草等法解讀徵兆，用於個人、農業、軍事或國家決策。重視不變爻預測穩定，異於周易的變爻。
- **目的**：哲學上推廣陰主宇宙觀，強調大地包容力、循環歸藏與和諧。在商脈絡，服務儀式與治理，融合神話解釋宇宙秩序。較周易的儒家道德教訓，神巫味更濃，連結祖先與自然力。目的實用（指導行動）與宇宙論（陰陽平衡），影響後世道家與民間傳統。

總之，歸藏提供窺探周前中國思想之窗，突出地勝於天，為理解古代占卜多樣性之關鍵。
