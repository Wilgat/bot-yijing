### Lian Shan (連山) in English

The **Lian Shan** (also known as **Lian Shan Yi** or "Linked Mountains" in English translation) is one of the three ancient Chinese divination texts referred to as the "Three Yi" (三易), alongside the **Gui Cang** (歸藏) and the **Zhou Yi** (周易, the I Ching or Book of Changes). It represents the earliest and most enigmatic tradition among the three, associated with themes of emergence, growth, and the mountains as sources of life, contrasting with the earth-centered Gui Cang and the heaven-earth balanced Zhou Yi. Below, I detail its background, history, current status, supporting evidence, objectives, and purpose, drawing from historical records, textual citations, and scholarly analyses. Unlike the Gui Cang, which has partial archaeological confirmation, the Lian Shan is entirely lost, making it a subject of speculation and reconstruction.

#### Background
The Lian Shan is rooted in prehistoric Chinese cosmology and divination, potentially dating to the Xia Dynasty (c. 2070–1600 BCE), though its existence is more legendary than proven. Traditional attributions link it to the divine sage Fu Xi (伏羲) or the Yellow Emperor (Huangdi), but it is primarily tied to the Xia era, hence its alternative name "Xia Yi" (夏易 or Summer Yi). The name "Lian Shan" evokes "linked mountains" or "mountains in succession," symbolizing the continuous emergence of all things from mountains, aligning with yang (陽) principles of initiation, elevation, and outward expansion. As explained by Han Dynasty scholar Zheng Xuan (鄭玄, 127–200 CE), it signifies "wanwu chu yu shan" (萬物出於山), emphasizing mountains as the origin of life and vitality, possibly reflecting Xia culture's focus on highland geography, ancestral mountains, and seasonal renewal.

The text purportedly consisted of 64 hexagrams (like the other Yi texts), but with a unique sequence starting with the Gen (艮, mountain) hexagram, rather than Qian (乾) in the Zhou Yi or Kun (坤) in the Gui Cang. Its divination method emphasized unchanging lines (7 and 8) for omens, similar to the Gui Cang, and its statements may have incorporated early myths or natural imagery, though no direct fragments survive. It likely integrated shamanistic elements, linking mountains to divine communication and cosmic order.

#### History
- **Ancient Origins and Transmission**: The Zhou Li (周禮 or Rites of Zhou) describes royal diviners using three Yi systems: Lian Shan (Xia), Gui Cang (Shang), and Zhou Yi (Zhou). It was supposedly the Xia Dynasty's primary divination manual, reflecting a society attuned to mountainous terrains and yang-driven cycles. By the Han Dynasty, Huan Tan (桓譚, c. 43 BCE–28 CE) noted it contained about 80,000 characters, making it the longest of the three. However, it survived only in sparse quotations in texts like the Taiping Yulan (太平御覽) and other encyclopedias, often in discussions of the Three Yi's relationships.

- **Loss and Fragmentary Survival**: The Lian Shan vanished earlier than the Gui Cang, likely by the late Han or Jin Dynasty (265–420 CE), with no complete copies mentioned after the Han. Song Dynasty scholars like Zhu Zhen (朱震) and Li Guo (李過) referenced it in works like Han Shang Yi Zhuan and Xi Xi Yi Shuo, but only to contrast it with the surviving Zhou Yi. No dedicated sections or myths (like those in Gui Cang) were preserved, leading to debates on its authenticity.

- **Qing Dynasty Reconstructions**: Qing scholars, including Ma Guohan (馬國翰), compiled scant fragments in Yuhan Shanfang Jiyi Shu (玉函山房輯佚書), gathering fewer than 100 characters from ancient sources. This remains the primary "transmitted version," though it is minimal compared to Gui Cang reconstructions.

- **Modern Scholarship**: No archaeological discoveries have confirmed the Lian Shan, unlike the 1993 Wangjiatai slips for Gui Cang. Research treats it as a theoretical or lost precursor, with studies like Wang Ning's (1995) on its nomenclature and Rui Magone's (1996) on its exegetical role.

#### Current Status
The Lian Shan is completely lost, with no surviving manuscripts or artifacts. Research depends on:
- Sparse textual fragments (e.g., Ma Guohan's compilation, under 100 characters).
- Indirect references in Han and later texts.
- Ongoing scholarly works: As of 2026, recent analyses include Xin Yamin's Gui Cang Yanjiu (2025), which contrasts it with Gui Cang, and digital compilations on platforms like Wikisource. No major breakthroughs since the 20th century, but comparative studies with oracle bones and early Yi variants continue. It is studied in Sinology and philosophy but not for practical divination, overshadowed by the Zhou Yi.

Debates persist on its existence: some view it as a Han-era invention to complete the "Three Yi" triad, while others see it as a genuine pre-Zhou tradition.

#### Supporting Evidence
- **Textual Citations**: Mentions in Zhou Li and Huan Tan's Xin Lun (新論) describe its Gen-first sequence and length, aligning with Three Yi frameworks. Quotations in Song texts (e.g., Zhu Zhen's discussions) match this.
- **Comparative Analyses**: Similarities with oracle bone divinations (Shang era) suggest early yang-mountain themes, though not direct proof. No archaeological evidence exists, unlike Gui Cang's bamboo slips.
- **Scholarly Consensus**: Works like Anke Hein's (2019) on textual authenticity use excavated Yi variants to argue Lian Shan's conceptual validity, even if lost. Traditional bibliographies (e.g., Morohashi) list it without dispute.
- **Lack of Counter-Evidence**: No records suggest forgery; its absence is attributed to early loss during dynastic transitions.

#### Objectives and Purpose
- **Objectives**: As a divination system (zhan shi, 占筮), it provided omens via yarrow stalks or similar methods for decisions in agriculture, warfare, and governance. It focused on unchanging lines for emergence predictions, emphasizing stability from origins.
- **Purpose**: Philosophically, it promoted a yang-centered cosmology, viewing mountains as sources of life and renewal, fostering harmony through outward growth. In Xia context (if real), it served ritual and state functions, integrating natural imagery for cosmic understanding. Its purpose was practical (guiding actions) and metaphysical (yang-yin initiation), possibly influencing later Daoist mountain cults, though less documented than the other Yi texts.

In essence, the Lian Shan embodies the speculative dawn of Chinese Yi traditions, symbolizing emergence from mountains, but its total loss makes it a phantom text for modern study.

### 連山（英文：Lian Shan）詳細解釋（繁體中文）

**連山**（又稱**連山易**）是中國古代「三易」之一，與**歸藏**（歸藏易）和**周易**（易經）並稱。它代表三易中最古老、最神秘的傳統，強調出發、生長與山岳作為生命源頭的主題，與地主歸藏、周易的天地平衡形成對比。以下詳細說明其背景、歷史、當前狀態、支持證據、目標與目的，基於歷史記載、文本引述及學術分析。與歸藏不同，連山完全亡佚，無考古實物，使其成為推測與重構的對象。

#### 背景
連山源於中國史前宇宙觀與占卜，潛在追溯至夏朝（約前2070–1600年），但其存在更屬傳說。傳統歸屬伏羲氏或黃帝，但主要與夏相關，故又名「夏易」。名稱「連山」意指「山連綿」或「山岳相連」，象徵萬物從山而出，體現陽（陽剛、出發、向上）的原則。漢代鄭玄解釋為「萬物出於山」，強調山岳作為生命起源，反映夏文化可能重視高原地理、祖山崇拜與季節更新。

文本據傳由64卦組成（與其他易相同），但獨特序以艮卦（山）開頭，而非周易的乾或歸藏的坤。其占法重視不變爻（七、八）為徵兆，卦辭可能融入早期神話或自然意象，但無直接殘片。它或許融合薩滿元素，將山岳連結神靈溝通與宇宙秩序。

#### 歷史
- **古代起源與傳承**：《周禮》記載周朝太卜掌三易：連山（夏）、歸藏（殷）、周易（周）。據傳為夏朝主要占卜手冊，反映重視山岳陽循環的社會。至漢代，桓譚《新論》記其約八萬言，為三易最長。但僅存零星引文於如《太平御覽》等百科。

- **亡佚與碎片存留**：較歸藏早亡，可能於晚漢或晉代（265–420年），後世無完整本提及。宋代學者如朱震、李過於《漢上易傳》、《西溪易說》提及，但僅用以對比周易。無專屬篇章或神話（如歸藏）保存，引發真實性辯論。

- **清代輯佚**：清學者如馬國翰於《玉函山房輯佚書》匯集不足百字碎片，為主要「傳世輯本」，但較歸藏輯佚少。

- **現代學術**：無考古發現確認，異於1993年王家台歸藏簡。研究視其為理論或亡佚前驅，如王寧（1995）卦名考、Rui Magone（1996）詮釋角色。

#### 當前狀態
連山完全亡佚，無存稿或文物。研究依賴：
- 零星文本碎片（如馬國翰輯，不足百字）。
- 漢及後世間接提及。
- 近期著作：至2026年，新分析如辛亞民《歸藏研究》（2025年）與其對比；數位彙編見維基文庫等。自20世紀無重大進展，但與甲骨文、早期易變體比較持續。它屬漢學與哲學研究，但不實占，隱於周易陰影。

存在辯論：有些視為漢代發明以成「三易」三元組，其他則視為真實周前傳統。

#### 支持證據
- **文本引述**：《周禮》與桓譚《新論》述其艮首序與長度，符合三易框架。宋文引述（如朱震論）與此吻合。
- **比較分析**：與甲骨占卜（殷代）類似陽山主題，雖非直接證。無考古證據，異於歸藏竹簡。
- **學術共識**：如Anke Hein（2019）文本真實性研究，用出土易變體論連山概念有效，即使亡佚。傳統書目（如森橋）無爭議列之。
- **無反證**：無記錄指偽造；亡佚歸因王朝轉變早期損失。

#### 目標與目的
- **目標**：作為占筮系統，用蓍草等法為農業、戰爭、治理決策提供徵兆。重視不變爻預測出發，強調起源穩定。
- **目的**：哲學上推廣陽主宇宙觀，將山視為生命源與更新，透過外向生長促和諧。若屬夏脈絡（若真），服務儀式與國家，融合自然意象解宇宙。其目的實用（指導行動）與形而上（陽陰起始），或影響後世道家山岳崇拜，雖記錄少於其他易。

總之，連山象徵中國易傳統推測曙光，代表從山出發，但完全亡佚使其成現代研究的幻影文本。
